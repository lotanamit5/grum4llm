\section{Paper Summary}

\subsection{
    Paper title and authors, where was it published?
}

The paper \textit{Preference Elicitation For General Random Utility Models} by Hossein Azari Soufiani, David C. Parkes, and Lirong Xia (SEAS, Harvard University) was published in AUAI 2013 (Association for Uncertainty in Artificial Intelligence).

\subsection{
    What is the topic of the paper?
    What is the main claim?
    How does this relate to the material we studied in the course?
}

The paper focuses on \textit{Preference Elicitation} within the framework of \textit{General Random Utility Models} (GRUMs).
The main claim of the paper is that its proposed adaptive elicitation scheme increases the precision of preference estimation in GRUMs.

This paper relates to the topic of \textit{Truth Discovery}, covered in later parts of the course.
Similar to the \textit{Mallows Model} learned in class, this paper's model assumes that agents' observed preferences are noisy signals generated from an underlying ground truth.
However, both models differ in the parametric assumptions they make and the form of their ground truth.
The Mallows Model assumes a \textbf{single} global ground truth preference, and the noise is \textbf{distance-based} from the ground truth (higher noise means the observed preference is more distant from the ground truth).
The GRUM model is \textbf{utility-based}, and assumes that utilities are influenced by the features of both agents and alternatives, meaning that each pair of agent and item has a unique ground truth utility, with the addition of an independent noise term.

\begin{definition}[General Random Utility Model - GRUM]
    Let $\mathcal{C}=\{ c_{1},\dots,c_{m} \}$ be a set of alternatives and a set of agents indexed by $i \in \{ 1,\dots,n \}$.
    For every agent $i$ and alternative $j$, the utility $u_{ij}$ is defined as:
    $$
    u_{ij} = \delta_{j} + \vec{x}_{i} B \vec{z}^{\top}_{j} + \epsilon_{ij}
    $$
    Where:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $x_{i} \in \mathbb{R}^K$ and $z_{j} \in \mathbb{R}^L$ are observed attribute vectors for agent $i$ and alternative $j$ (correspondingly).
        \item $\Theta=(\delta,B)$ are the parameters to be estimated.
        \begin{itemize}
        	\item $\delta \in \mathbb{R}^{m}$ is the vector of intrinsic utilities.
        	\item $B \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times L}$ is the vector intersection matrix.
        \end{itemize}
    	\item $\epsilon_{ij}$ is a random noise term.
        \item Denote the deterministic term as $\mu_{ij} = \delta_{j} + \vec{x}_{i} B \vec{z}^{\top}_{j}$.
    \end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Preference Data]
    Let $D = \{ \pi_{1},\dots,\pi_{n} \}$ be the set of observed rankings,
    where $\pi_{i}$ represents the full ranking of agent $i$ such that $c_{\pi_{i}(1)} \succ_{i} \cdots \succ_{i} c_{\pi_{i}(m)}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Identifiability]
    The linear model describing the deterministic utility, $\mu_{ij} = \delta_{j} + \vec{x}_{i} B \vec{z}^{\top}_{j}$, in terms of parameters $\Theta$ is \textit{identifiable} if the mapping from parameters to utilities is injective (one-to-one).
    That is, distinct parameters yield distinct deterministic utilities.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Location Family Assumption]
    The noise terms $\epsilon_{ij}$ are drawn from a distribution where the probability density function is log-concave (e.g., Gumbel or Normal).
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Global Optimality] \label{thm:global_optimality}
For the location family, if for every $i \leq n$ the joint probability density function for $\vec{\epsilon}_i$ and the prior $\mathrm{Pr}(\Theta)$ are log-concave, 
then  $\mathrm{Pr}(\Theta \mid D)$ is concave up to a known transformation.
\end{theorem}

\begin{note}
By \textit{up to a known transformation}, the authors meant that the posterior is log-concave (the "known transformation" is the natural logarithm function).
Also, the original Theorem \ref{thm:global_optimality} from the paper had a typo; it should have been \textit{every $i \leq n$} instead of \textit{every $j \leq m$} because we require that each agent's noise vector is drawn from a log-concave distribution.
\end{note}

\begin{remark}
    Standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms suffer from the problem of getting stuck in local minima.
    Theorem \ref{thm:global_optimality} guarantees that the posterior is unimodal, ensuring that gradient-based inference methods will converge to the global maximum.
    In short, the theorem ensures the answer is \textbf{correct}.
\end{remark}

\begin{condition}[Connectivity]
\label{cond:connectivity}
    Given the data $D$, in every partition of the alternatives $C$ into two non-empty subsets $C_{1} \cup C_{2}$, 
    there exists $c_{1}\in C_{1}$ and $c_{2} \in C_{2}$ such that there is at least one ranking in $D$ where $c_{1} \succ c_{2}$.    
\end{condition}

\begin{theorem}[Boundedness of Solutions] \label{thm:boundedness_of_solutions}
    Suppose we fix $\mu_{11} = 0$.
    Then the set $S_{D}$ of global maxima solutions to $\mathrm{Pr}(\Theta \mid D)$ is bounded in $\Theta$ if and only if the data $D$ satisfies Condition \ref{cond:connectivity} 
    and the linear model describing $\mu$ in terms of $\Theta$ is identifiable.
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}
    Theorem \ref{thm:boundedness_of_solutions} provides the necessary and sufficient conditions to ensure the solution set is bounded (see Fig. \ref{fig:fig1}).
    It generalizes Ford's condition for Plackett-Luce (\cite{ford1957solution}) to the broader class of GRUMs.
    In short, the theorem ensures the answer is \textbf{computable}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{

}

The original paper omitted the proofs for the theorem, and I was unable to find a version of the paper with the appendix of the proofs.
After contacting the authors, they referred me to the Phd thesis of the main author (\cite{soufiani2014revisiting}).
Theorems 1 and 2 in the thesis can be generalized to prove the above theorems (correspondingly).

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem \ref{thm:global_optimality}]
    Theorem \ref{thm:global_optimality} is proved by applying the following lemma:
    \begin{lemma} \label{lem:1}
        Let $R \in \mathbb{R}$. Suppose $g_{1}(\vec{\Theta},\vec{\epsilon}),\dots,g_{R}(\vec{\Theta},\vec{\epsilon})$ are concave functions in $\mathbb{R}^{m(2n+1)}$ where $\vec{\Theta}$ is the vector of $m+mn$ parameters and $\vec{\epsilon}$ is a vector of $mn$ real numbers that are generated according to a distribution whose pdf is logarithmic concave in $\mathbb{R}^{mn}$. Then the following function is log-concave in $\mathbb{R}^{m}$:
        \begin{equation*}
        L_{i} (\vec{\Theta},G) =
        \mathrm{Pr} \left[
          g_{1}(\vec{\Theta},\vec{\epsilon}) \geq 0,\dots,g_{R}(\vec{\Theta},\vec{\epsilon}) \geq 0
        \right],\quad 
        \vec{\Theta} \in \mathbb{R}^{m(n+1)}
        \end{equation*}
    \end{lemma}
    
    A ranking $\pi_{i}$ implies that the item at rank $r$, denoted $j=\pi_{i}(r)$, is preferred to the item at rank $r+1$denoted $k=\pi_{i}(r+1)$.
    This means the utility of the higher-ranked item is greater:
    $$
    U_{\pi_{i}(r)} \geq  U_{\pi_{i}(r+1)}
    $$
    We can substitute this with the GRUM definition:
    $$
    \delta_{j} + x_{i} B z^{\top}_{j} + \epsilon_{ij} \geq 
    \delta_{k} + x_{i} B z^{\top}_{k} + \epsilon_{ik}
    $$
    Lemma \ref{lem:1} requires the problem to be framed as probability constraints in the format $g(\Theta,\epsilon) \geq 0$.
    We rearrange the inequality above and define:
    \begin{align*}
    g^{i}_{r}(\Theta,\epsilon) 
    &= U_{\pi_{i}(r)} -  U_{\pi_{i}(r+1)} \\
    &= \delta_{j} + x_{i} B z^{\top}_{j} + \epsilon_{ij} - 
    \delta_{k} - x_{i} B z^{\top}_{k} - \epsilon_{ik} \\
    &= (\delta_{j}- \delta_{k}) + x_{i} B (z_{j} - z_{k})^{\top} + (\epsilon_{ij} - \epsilon_{ik}) \geq 0
    \end{align*}
    The function $g^{i}_{r}$ represents the pairwise comparison between rank $r$ and $r+1$.
    Lemma \ref{lem:1} applies only if the functions $g$ are \textbf{concave}.
    \begin{itemize}
        \item It's linear in $\delta$ because its a simple difference.
        \item It's linear in $B$ because $x,z$ are observed data (constants), so it's the same as multiplying by some constant $C$. 
    \end{itemize}
    Since all linear functions are concave, the condition is satisfied.
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item The noise $\epsilon$ is assumed to have a \textbf{log-concave PDF} (e.g., Normal or Gumbel).
        \item The constraints $g$ are concave.
        \item Therefore, by Lemma \ref{lem:1}, the probability of observing a single ranking $\mathrm{Pr}(\pi_{i}|\Theta)$ is log-concave.
        \item Since the sum of concave is also concave, the total likelihood, $\mathrm{Pr}(D \mid \Theta)$, is log-concave.
    \end{enumerate}
    We also assumed that the prior, $\mathrm{Pr}(\Theta)$, is log-concave.
    This implies that the posterior, $\mathrm{Pr}(\Theta \mid D) \propto \mathrm{Pr}(D \mid \Theta) \cdot \mathrm{Pr}(\Theta)$, is log-concave.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem \ref{thm:boundedness_of_solutions}]
    Theorem \ref{thm:boundedness_of_solutions}'s proof uses the following lemma:
    \begin{lemma} \label{lem:2}
        If alternative $j$ is preferred to alternative $j'$ in at least one ranking then the difference of their mean parameters $\theta_{j'} - \theta_{j}$ is bounded from above ($\exists Q:\theta_{j'}-\theta_{j}<Q$) for all the $\vec{\theta}$ that maximize the likelihood function.
    \end{lemma}
    If distinct sets of parameters $\Theta$ and $\Theta'$ yield the exact same utilities $\mu$, the model cannot distinguish between them, and the solution set might be unbounded (e.g., along a line).
    Additionally, if Condition \ref{cond:connectivity} does not hold, then $S_{D}$ is unbounded because the parameters for all alternatives in $C_{1}$ can be increased simultaneously to improve the log-likelihood.
    Consider a directed graph $G_{D}$, where the nodes are the alternatives, and there is edge from $c_{j}$ to $c_{j'}$ if in a least one ranking $c_{j} \succ c_{j'}$. By Condition \ref{cond:connectivity}, for any pair $j\neq j'$, there is a path from $c_{j}$ to $c_{j'}$ (and conversely, a path from $c_{j'}$ to $c_{j}$).
    To see this, consider building a path between $j$ and $j'$ by starting from a partition with $C_{1} = \{ j \}$ and following an edge from $j$ to $j_{1}$ in the graph where $j_{1}$ is an alternative in $C_{2}$ for which there must be such an edge, by Condition \ref{cond:connectivity}.
    Consider the partition with $C_{1}=\{ j,j_{1} \}$, and repeat until an edge can be followed to vertex $j' \in C_{2}$.
    It follows from Lemma \ref{lem:2} that for any $\vec{\theta} \in S_{D}$ we have $\left| \theta_{j}  - \theta_{j'} \right| < Qm$, using the telescopic sum of bounded values of the difference of mean parameters along the edges of the path, since the length of the path is no more than $m$ (and tracing the path from $j$ to $j'$ and $j'$ to $j$), meaning that $S_{D}$ is bounded.
\end{proof}

To clarify the implications of Theorem \ref{thm:boundedness_of_solutions}, I generated Figure \ref{fig:fig1},
which visualizes the relationship between data connectivity (Condition \ref{cond:connectivity}) and the stability of the solution.
GRUM estimates parameters ($\Theta$) based on the difference in utilities between alternatives.
If the data violates Condition \ref{cond:connectivity} (as shown in the left graph of Figure \ref{fig:fig1}), 
the alternatives are partitioned into disconnected sets, for example, $\{A, B\}$ never compete against $\{C\}$.
Because the model relies on relative comparisons, there is no data to 'anchor' the utility of set $\{C\}$ against the others.
The contour plot on the bottom left illustrates the consequence of this violation:
the log-likelihood function forms an 'infinite ridge' rather than a peak.
The white arrow demonstrates that we can increase the parameter $\theta_C$ indefinitely (shifting it to $+\infty$ or $-\infty$) without changing the likelihood of the observed data.
This shows that the solution set $S_D$ is unbounded, meaning the algorithm cannot converge to a unique answer.
In contrast, when the data satisfies Condition \ref{cond:connectivity} (right graph), the comparison graph is strongly connected.
This connectivity constrains the parameters relative to each other.
As seen in the bottom right contour plot, this collapses the ridge into a strictly concave function with a single, bounded global maximum (the red dot),
guaranteeing that the MAP estimate is unique and computable.

}

The paper \textit{Automobile Prices in Market Equilibrium} (\cite{berry1993automobile}) (often referred to as the BLP model) was cited as an important paper because the authors explicitly define GRUMs as a specific case of this influential generative model from econometrics.
This cite establishes the theoretical foundation of the work, while distinguishing GRUMs as a simplified version that relies only on observed attributes to ensure the computational tractability that the original BLP model lacked.
